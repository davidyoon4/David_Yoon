Putting The Social
Back In Social Media

Context

Instagram has recently become an addicting, superficial, numbers-obsessed game that many people play. During our time in Silicon Valley, a team of 4 students analyzed why this was happening – and how to fix it.
Timeline
Jan - Mar 2020
Roles
UX Researcher, UX Designer
Outcomes
2 new Instagram feature concepts, Medium article

Research

We conducted interviews on tech professionals, teens, and parents on their experiences with this tech giant in the technology hub of the country. Along with screen time reports, articles on gambling, and much more, we found these takeaways.
People do NOT like to feel left out of things
Some features are based on slot machines – designed to pull you in
Instagram can cause episodes of depression and anxiety

Research – cont.

We were able to find insights like these through the following methods.

Interviewing employees from Apple, Facebook, startup founders
Student/teenage user interviews and testing
Reading past redesigns and articles about Instagram

Human Psychology Exploits

- FOMO (Fear of missing out)
- Our yearn for social validation
- Social comparison
- Desire for a certain 'aesthetic'
- Dopamine rush from refreshing the Explore page (slot machine)

Drawbacks of High Social Media Usage

- Depressive symptoms
- Anxiety
- Unproductivity & Guilt
- Physical deterioration
- Paradox of choices (decision fatigue)

Prototypes

We then moved on to ideating and creating low fidelity prototypes. Since we were redesigning Instagram and not overhauling it, we decided to make many of our prototypes by overlaying features on screenshots of the current Instagram app.

User Testing

After developing some initial prototypes, we interviewed some fellow social media addicts – the 23 other members in our Bay Area program cohort. They were able to provide feedback and criticism that pushed our designs forward.

User Testing – cont.

We heard...

“I only want to see stories of my actual friends. Not people that I followed in middle school and am too afraid to unfollow now.”
“I clicked on 1 TikTok video once and now my whole explore page is full of them.”
“I like the option of making it collaborative, but maybe think about how to name and display it clearly on their pages.”

...so we iterated!

Close Friends feature: filter and prioritize whose content you see, and who sees your content.
‘Scrapbooks’ – a unified, collaborative photo gallery shared by multiple people.
Granted user ability to edit their Explore algorithm of their interests.

Outcomes

We gathered feedback from testing and used it to iterate on our designs. We focused on three features: Close Friends, Scrapbooks, and reducing addictive mechanisms. Check out the Medium article we wrote for more insight to our process!

Outcomes – cont.

Close Friends

Instead of choosing who can see your content, you’re selecting whose content you can see. This way, you create the list by pulling from the people you follow, not the people who follow you.

Reducing Addictiveness

A revamped Explore page combats mindless, endless scorlling, while a self-curated interest profile provides content that is chosen by YOU – nobody, or nothing else.

Scrapbooks

Scrapbooks promote creation over consumption by taking the social interactions of real life and translating them online, providing genuine unity in a primarily superifical environment.

Reflection

This was the first product redesign I was apart of. I enjoyed the challenge of working with existing guidelines and standards – especially with as well-established of a platform as Instagram! Our team of 5 had a lot of skills at hand, we just needed to figure out how to apply them with the best synergy possible.
Pat on the Back
Creating prototypes that clearly communicate the design and are visually appealing/engaging
Lessons Learned
Teamwork without a designated team lead – dividing work critiquing it
Moving Forward
