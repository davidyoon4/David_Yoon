The Trunk
How might we make moving at NU about new beginnings, not sad endings?

Context

Moving on college campuses is often expensive, time-consuming, and wasteful. A team of Northwestern students explored how to make moving accessible, collaborative, and something to look forward to.
Timeline
Jan - Mar 2020
Roles
UX Researcher, UX Designer
Outcomes
Interactive mobile prototype

Our Solution

We gathered feedback from testing and used it to iterate on our designs. We focused on streamlining the entire moving sequence away from the students and towards a third-party middleman. The resultant app became The Trunk – a hybrid digital and physical moving truck designed specifically for moving days at Northwestern.

Research

We started by investigating the main existing solutions that students use during moving the seasons. We also interviewed and surveyed Northwestern students to gain insights into some prevalent challenges and workarounds they have found.
People will always take the path of least resistance
Large furniture is thrown out if not sold on time
Waste, moving vans, and buying furniture are all expensive

Research – cont.

Existing Solution: Facebook Free & For Sale (F&FS)

Flawed search mechanics. Search for “chair” results in listing for clock, sofa, and bed
Alert for user's sold items is not very visible. Many items are unavailable and the buyer has no way of knowing
Multiple items at different price points are listed, but only 1 price is shown at the top for $15

Prototypes

We conducted interviews on tech professionals, teens, and parents on their experiences with this tech giant in the technology hub of the country. Along with screen time reports, articles on gambling, and much more, we found these takeaways.
Dumpstr Dive focused on how to introduce a nature of circular economy into furniture at NU: how to best pass down items from one person to the next
Last Call also focused on sustainability but accepts the fact that some items won’t get sold. These items would be collected and distributed to local donation centers

Prototypes – cont.

We combined these ideas into 1 web application prototype that takes the best parts of both ideas.

Journey Maps

BUYER – before using The Trunk. The buyer must browse F&FS until they find an item they like. However, there’s no guarantee that the item is available, sold for the listed price, or even that the seller will message back.

Seller – before using The Trunk. They often get no interest for their items, and the messages they do get are likely lowball offers.

All – after using The Trunk. Much of the burden of negotiating and interacting is streamlined and abstracted away from the students.

User Testing

After developing some initial prototypes, we interviewed students at NU from different ages, locations, and distances from campus. We also surveyed Northwestern students that provided criticism and feedback that pushed our designs forward. These were our key takeaways:
Physically moving items is the biggest barrier to moving
Connecting buyer and seller is slow and tedious – a major bottleneck
Students are always looking for items, and prefer to buy them used

User Testing – cont.

Research and testing methods

Survey for all NU students
Interviewing other students
User walkthroughs

Through this feedback, we found that although there were some pain points with buying and selling items on Free & For Sale, the biggest problem students were having came afterwards. Coordinating the logistics of communicating with the buyer/seller and physically picking up the item were much more prevalent roadblocks.

Pivot

Rather than redesigning the user flows of F&FS, we moved towards streamlining the entire process away from the user to bypass many of the pain points we identified. We used the insights to wireframe a new design.

Reflection

Although not the first White Space challenge I've done, this one provided new and interesting challenges. Being physically removed from the space we were designing for really forced us to flex our empathizing muscles and document our ideas well. It was exciting to design something that I was potentially a primary stakeholder in!
Pat on the Back
Not falling in love with our work and being able to pivot towards a solution that was supported by our research
Lessons Learned
Exploring spaces in our lives to identify and define problems
Moving Forward
Working with NU's startup incubator to learn how to implement an app into the community
